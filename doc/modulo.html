Modular arithmetic

The modulo operator returns the remainder of a division. For example, the expression "31mod  1031\mod 1031mod10" would evaluate to 111, because 313131 divided by 101010 is 333 with a remainder of 111.

The LCG formula uses the modulo operator to calculate the next random number xn+1x_{n+1}xn+1​ using the previous one xnx_nxn​:

xn+1=7xnmod  10 \begin{aligned} x_{n+1} &= 7x_n \mod 10 \\ \end{aligned} xn+1​​=7xn​mod10​

The value 777 is called the multiplier, and the value 101010 is called the modulus. (These values are just examples).

Let's look at the output for various seed values x0x_0x0​.

Seed x0=0x_0=0x0​=0

xnx_nxn​ 7xn7x_n7xn​ mod  10\mod 10mod10
000 000 000
000 000 000
...

Obviously 000 is a poor choice and something we must avoid.

Seed x0=1x_0=1x0​=1

xnx_nxn​ 7xn7x_n7xn​ mod  10\mod 10mod10
111 777 777
777 494949 999
999 636363 333
333 212121 111
111 777 777
...

The sequence repeats after 444 iterations. We say that the period is 444.

Seed x0=2x_0=2x0​=2

xnx_nxn​ 7xn7x_n7xn​ mod  10\mod 10mod10
222 141414 444
444 282828 888
888 565656 666
666 424242 222
222 141414 444
...

The period is 4.

Note that the output is always less than the modulus, or this case less than 101010.

Also note that the only number we haven't seen yet is 555, so let's try 555 as the initial seed...

Seed x0=5x_0=5x0​=5

The period is 111 so obviously 555 is a poor choise as well.
